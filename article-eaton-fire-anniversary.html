One Year After the Eaton Fire: Survivors Still Face Uphill Battle for Recovery

The first anniversary of the devastating Eaton Fire in Altadena is a stark reminder that wildfire recovery is not a one-year journey—it’s a long, complex path that many survivors are still navigating today. Despite heroic community efforts, persistent barriers stand in the way of real recovery for those who lost homes, stability, and loved ones.

By Wildland Fire Recovery Fund January 7, 2026 4 min read

The Ongoing Struggle for Fire Survivors

  • Delayed insurance payouts have stranded families in limbo, unable to rebuild or return home.
  • Many fire victims remain at risk of homelessness, shuffling between temporary housing options—some as many as 10 to 15 times.
  • Federal and state resources have not matched the scale of the community’s need, leaving critical gaps for thousands of families.
“Whether a family is able to get back home or is displaced permanently comes down to one thing: Money.”

Personal Stories of Resilience and Loss

  • Zaire Calvin’s family, lifelong residents of Altadena, still mourns the loss of his sister—and now faces eviction instead of healing.
  • Ada Hernandez and her family, including two small children, still have no permanent housing a year after losing their home.
  • Survivors like Casty Fortich rely on flexible employers and donated essentials, demonstrating both the resilience and the precariousness of fire-impacted families.

Recovery Barriers: Beyond the Flames

  • Nonprofits, disaster relief programs, and the SBA have become lifelines, but their hours and resources often fall short for working families.
  • Survivor stories reveal a broken system: recovery paperwork, insurance negotiations, and basic survival tasks are hard to juggle when there’s nowhere certain to sleep.
“How can someone complete paperwork, think of rebuilding, take care of their children, and hold down a job when they don’t even know where they’re going to sleep tonight?”

The Call for Action

  • Fairness and accountability in insurance practices
  • Targeted federal disaster relief to meet actual needs
  • Ongoing local and state support for long-term recovery
“The scale of needs dwarves the resources the county and state have already provided.”

Community, Compassion, and the Path Forward

A year after the Eaton Fire, the need for sustained support—financial, emotional, and structural—has never been clearer. The Wildland Fire Recovery Fund stands with Altadena and all fire-impacted communities, committed to driving change, mobilizing resources, and amplifying survivor voices for as long as it takes.

Together, we can transform loss into hope, and build systems that ensure no survivor is left behind. Join us in supporting recovery, resilience, and justice for those affected by wildfires.
